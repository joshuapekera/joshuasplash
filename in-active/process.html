Inspiration

 

When I decided to apply to Luckie I knew I would need to make an impression. So what would impress people at Luckie? A few days after deciding I would apply, I read a blog post by Luckie's Director of Human Resources, Melissa Wheeler. The post was entitled, "Applying For a Job? Here's What Not to Do". The article was full of common sense tips for your typical recent college graduate. Even though I am far from being a recent college graduate I found the post very helpful. There was something Melissa said that caught my attention. She said candidates should,

 
 
“Follow our blog, check our website – do some homework. I love candidates who make an effort to tailor their resume to a specific job position and tell us how their skills will benefit the agency.”
 
 

My initial inspiration to create this product came to me after reading that post. What better way to apply for a position? This product is my resume, but it also is an example of everything my resume says I know how to do. It is specifically tailored for the position I want at Luckie and it illustrates how my skills will benefit the agency.

 

Product Design

UX Design

A top notch user experience does not happen by accident. UX design encompasses a plethora of disciplines. Team members should all have a basic knowledge of each discipline and how all are effected by their area of expertise. I work through a process that is multilevel. It is an iterative process that thrives in a team setting by focusing on Strategy, Scope, Structure, Skeleton and Surface. Each level builds on the previous one, but allows for overlap in order to refine a product until completion. I would be happy to discuss this process in more detail with anyone that is interested. Resources & Credit

I am an advocate of open source software. I try to contribute as often as I can to open source initiatives. Most developers establish their own standard methods and tools they like to use in their products. I am no different than most. Here is a list of some of the tools I used in this product. Please take a look and experiment with some of them.

HTML5 Boilerplate

The primary goal of Boilerplate is to help a developer deliver a solid user experience across any OS, browser and device. It is a collection of best practices for HTML5. It helps a developer establish a solid foundation for a new product. It includes CSS resets, graceful degradation for legacy browsers, cross-browser fixes, .htaccess configurations and a whole lot more.

Check it out ›

Twitter Bootstrap

Bootstrap is a starting library of HTML, CSS and Javascript resources. A number of things are packed into the framework, but the most useful is the out of the box configuration for fluid development. Responsive web design requires an iterative design process. Bootstrap is ideal for this because you can begin prototyping and testing content from day one of a project.

Check it out ›

Animate.css

The days of fully Flash sites are over. We have little need for it thanks to the explosion of mobile browsing and progressive browsers embracing HTML5 and CSS3 standards. Software driven animations on a mobile device using javascript and jQuery are not the answer. They perform poorly on a mobile device with sluggish and choppy results. However, hardware accelerated animations using 2D and 3D transforms perform beautifully. Animate.css is a library of keyframe animations. I have written some add-ons to compliment the default CSS provided. I recommend using this library when it can add some subtle elegance to your product. Keep in mind their are some draw backs to using 2D and 3D transforms. Much of it is sparsely documented, but I would love to share what I have learned.
